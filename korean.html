Interview with June, a year 4 Korean student

June, a fourth-year Cognitive Science major at UCSD from Seoul, Korea, shares her experiences as an international student. She chose UCSD for its size and reputation as a large public university in the U.S. However, as an East Asian student, she feels pressure to conform to the stereotype that Asians are academically high-achieving, particularly in subjects like math. This has influenced her decision to take higher-level math courses, even though she feels burdened by these expectations. Despite this, June believes that UCSD lacks a strong sense of community for East Asian students, and resources are limited unless actively sought out.

June has also experienced instances of prejudice and discomfort, particularly in class discussions and group projects. She recalls a moment when a professor referred to "international students who can't speak English," which made her feel uncomfortable. Furthermore, she feels that class examples often focus on American contexts, making it harder for international students to relate. In group projects, she feels the weight of being an international student and often works harder to avoid being perceived as a hindrance. Additionally, she has noticed that TAs sometimes speak slowly to her, assuming she might struggle with English, which she finds discriminatory.

June suggests that UCSD could improve its support for international students by providing more consistent and clear resources beyond initial orientation. She advocates for workshops and programs that help international students secure jobs in the U.S. after graduation and believes the school should offer more inclusive programs that cater to the diversity of its student body. Overall, June feels that UCSD could do more to create a more inclusive environment for international students, both academically and socially.
